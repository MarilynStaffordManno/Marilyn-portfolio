Case Study

Discover

Making financial products feel human and approachable—translating complex banking into language that builds trust and reduces anxiety.

Role
Lead Content Designer
Timeline
2019 – 2020
Scope
Financial UX, Accessibility, Product Content
[Project Hero Image]

The Challenge

Financial services content is notoriously bad. It's full of jargon, legal disclaimers, and language designed to protect the company rather than help the customer. Discover wanted to be different—to feel like a bank that actually talks to you like a person.

The challenge: simplify without dumbing down. Make complex financial products understandable while maintaining accuracy and compliance.

"People don't distrust banks because of interest rates. They distrust banks because of how banks talk to them."

The Approach

I partnered with product, legal, and compliance teams to find the line between clear and accurate. The goal wasn't to hide complexity—it was to present it in a way that felt manageable.

Key initiatives included:

  • Rewriting account opening flows in plain language
  • Creating a glossary system that explained terms in context
  • Developing accessible content patterns for screen readers
  • Designing error messages that actually helped people fix problems

[Contextual Help System]

Accessibility Focus

Financial exclusion often starts with content. If someone can't understand their bank account, they can't manage their money effectively. We paid special attention to accessibility—not just technical compliance, but genuine usability for people with different needs and literacy levels.

[Accessibility Audit Results]

The Outcome

The plain language initiative reduced customer service calls related to confusion and improved completion rates on key flows. Accessibility improvements helped Discover exceed WCAG guidelines and reach more customers effectively.

Reflection

Working in financial services taught me that trust is built in small moments. Every time you use a word someone doesn't understand, you lose a little trust. Every time you make them feel stupid, you lose more. The goal isn't to sound smart—it's to make your customer feel smart.
